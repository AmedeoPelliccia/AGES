# RFC-0010 — GTL: Generative Transitive Language

**Status:** Draft · **Document class:** RFC proposal · **Repository:** AGES
**Authors:** AGES contributors · **Created:** 2026-07-22

## Abstract

This RFC proposes GTL — Generative Transitive Language — as a
functional engine and exploratory language model within AGES: a formal
or semi-formal language for representing grounded transitive action
candidates, positioned between structured semantic representation and
validated cyber-physical or computational execution.

## Motivation

A negotiated semantic artefact declares *what is intended*; nothing in
AGES yet represents *what bounded and verifiable operation could
realise it* on an identified object. Free-form plans are not
inspectable, not boundable and not adjudicable. GTL fills this gap
without becoming an execution mechanism.

## Transitivity

GTL restricts itself to transitive operations: every verb takes an
identified direct object. Intransitive or unanchored behaviour ("be
robust", "improve") belongs to the semantic layer, not to GTL.

## Executor–verb–object binding

A GTL expression binds an identified executor, a transitive operation
and an identified direct object. The executor may be a human, agent,
service, controller, machine or composite system, acting under
declared, delegated authority.

## Operational context

Each expression declares the system, environment and operating mode in
which the candidate is meaningful; a candidate is not portable across
contexts without re-grounding.

## Preconditions

Preconditions are explicit, individually identified, and where possible
tied to evidence references, so their satisfaction is checkable before
authorisation and again before execution.

## Limits

The admissible operational envelope declares temporal, physical,
computational and authority limits. Execution outside the envelope is
by definition unauthorised.

## Effectivity

Every candidate declares effectivity — organisations, programmes,
products, instances, environments and validity interval — consistent
with [`../architecture/04-effectivity.md`](../architecture/04-effectivity.md).

## Failure semantics

Each candidate declares abort conditions, a safe state, compensation
actions and, where applicable, a rollback target. Physically
irreversible actions require explicit representation of that
irreversibility.

## Closure evidence

Each candidate declares the signals, verification method, tolerance,
timeout and required attestation by which operational closure is
demonstrated
([`../schemas/examples/closure-evidence.example.yaml`](../schemas/examples/closure-evidence.example.yaml),
non-normative). Closure evidence precedes any dependent baseline
ratification.

## Formal versus semi-formal profiles

GTL may admit profiles: a semi-formal profile (structured fields with
natural-language expressions) and a formal profile (machine-checkable
expressions admitting static analysis or formal verification). The
required degree of formality per domain is an open question.

## Physical and computational grounding

Direct objects may be physical, computational, informational or
organisational. Grounding requires an identified object, not a class:
"a valve" is not groundable; `asset:valve-CV-118` is.

## Authority boundaries

GTL produces an executable action candidate, not an automatically
executable command. Technical executability is not permission to
execute. A candidate becomes executable only after validation,
adjudication and authorisation. Where neural generation produces GTL
expressions, generation is separated from independent deterministic
validation.

## Alternatives considered

Reusing existing domain command languages directly (rejected: no
uniform representation of limits, authority and closure evidence);
free-form structured plans (rejected: not boundable); making GTL a
runtime executor (rejected: conflates specification with execution and
bypasses governance).

## Compatibility impact

Additive. GTL does not replace domain-specific command languages or
safety-critical certification; it precedes validation and adjudication
and never follows ratification. No broader GES superclass is
introduced.

## Unresolved questions

Actions affecting multiple direct objects; concurrent actions and
dependencies; atomic, transactional and compensating semantics;
physically irreversible actions; the degree of formal verification per
profile; uncertainty propagation from GENTILE; authorisation of
alternative candidate sets
([`../research/open-questions.md`](../research/open-questions.md)).

## Decision record
